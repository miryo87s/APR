and Its Causal Interpretation

OLS Estimator of Treatment Effects


Consider a common scenario in policy evaluation:

For each individual \(i\) we observe

  • A binary treatment/policy \(D_i\)
  • Some outcome variable \(Y_i\)
  • Control variables/Covariates \(X_i\)

To estimate the treatment effect, economists often consider OLS estimator of \(\tau_{ols}\) in \[ Y_i = \tau_{ols} D_i + X_i \beta + \epsilon_i, \quad \quad \mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i|D_i, X_i] = 0 \]

But what is the causal interpretation of \(\tau_{ols}\)?

Preview of Results


  • A general causal interpretation of \(\tau_{ols}\) under heterogeneous treatment effect
    • Under standard assumptions
    • Build connection between OLS and Inverse Propensity Weighting (IPW)
    • OLS + linear propensity score = Weighted IPW
    • OLS identifies overlap-weighted ATE
  • OLS is inferior to IPW
  • OLS identification relies on correct specification of \(\mathbb{E}[D_i|X_i]\)


  • Investigate several estimators of OLS and IPW
  • Illustrate results with an application to the NSW-CPS data (LaLonde 1986)

Literature Review

Interpretation of \(\tau_{ols}\) in special cases

  • Imbens and Rubin (2015): In randomized experiments, \(\tau_{ols}\) is ATE
  • Angrist and Pischke (2009): Under homogeneous treatment effect, \(\tau_{ols}\) is ATE

Under heterogeneous treatment effect

  • Angrist (1998): \(\tau_{ols}\) is a convex combination of ATEs in strata defined by discrete \(X_i\)

  • Similar causal interpretation is given by Aronow and Samii (2016)

    • \(\tau_{ols}\) is a weighted average of individual treatment effects \[ \tau_{ols} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[w_i\tau_i]}{\mathbb{E}[w_i]} \] where \(\tau_i = Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)\) is the individual treatment effect.
    • No connection to IPW or properties of \(w_i\)
    • Incorrect generalization to categorical and continuous \(D_i\)

Literature Review

Under heterogeneous treatment effect

  • Słoczyński (2022): \[ \tau_{ols} = w \times \tau_{APLE, 1} + (1 - w) \times \tau_{APLE, 0} \]

    • \(\tau_{APLE, d}\) is the projection of potential outcome \(Y_i(d)\) on \(\mathbb{E}[D_i|X_i]\)
    • \(\tau_{APLE, d}\) is causal if \(Y_i(d)\) is a linear function of \(\mathbb{E}[D_i|X_i]\) (restrictive assumption)
    • \(w\) is decreasing in the size of the treated group
    • I use standard assumptions and the weights have similar properties


  • Kline (2011): Oaxaca-Blinder (or full-interaction regression) is equivalent to IPW
    • Assumes that conditional treatment odds is linear in \(X_i\)
    • Also shows OB is doubly robust
    • Unclear when without interactions

Literature Review

OLS with multiple treatment

  • Goldsmith-Pinkham, Hull, and Kolesár (2022) \[ Y_i = \tau_1D_{1i} + \tau_2D_{2i} + X_i\beta + \epsilon_i \]
    • OLS estimators \(\hat\tau_1\) and \(\hat\tau_2\) are biased even in randomized experiments
    • I focus on the case with one binary treatment


IPW and weighted IPW

  • Rosenbaum and Rubin (1983); Robins and Rotnitzky (1995); Hahn (1998) and many others
  • A class of weighted IPW was first proposed by Hirano, Imbens, and Ridder (2003)
  • Li, Morgan, and Zaslavsky (2018) proposed overlap-weighted IPW
    • I show OLS is overlap-weighted IPW with linear propensity

The Potential Outcome Framework


The potential outcome framework (Rubin 1974)

  • Potential outcomes: \(Y_i(1)\)/\(Y_i(0)\) if unit \(i\) were treated/untreated
  • Binary treatment \(D_i\)
  • Observed outcome: \(Y_i = Y_i(1)D_i + Y_i(0)(1 - D_i)\)
  • We observe data \((Y_i, D_i, X_i)\)


Two standard assumptions to identify treatment effect

  1. Unconfoundedness \(D_i \perp (Y_i(1), Y_i(0)) | X_i\)

  2. Overlap \(0 < P(D_i = 1|X_i) < 1\)

IPW Identification of Treatment Effects

Define the ATE: \(\tau_{ate} = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(1) - Y_i(0)]\)

Under the unconfounded and overlap assumptions,

Let \(e(X_i) = P(D_i = 1|X_i)\) be the propensity score. The identification strategy of IPW is \[ \tau_{ipw} = \mathbb{E}\left[\underbrace{\frac{Y_iD_i}{e(X_i)} - \frac{Y_i(1 - D_i)}{1 - e(X_i)}}_{\delta_i := }\right] = \tau_{ate} \]

Further extended by Hirano, Imbens, and Ridder (2003) for ATE in a subpopulation defined by weight \(h(X_i)\)

\[ \text{Weighted IPW}: \tau_{wipw} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(X_i)}{\mathbb{E}[h(X_i)]} \cdot \delta_i\right] \]

All of weighted IPW estimators have a causal interpretation in the sense that \[ h(X_i) > 0 \ \forall X_i \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(X_i)}{\mathbb{E}[h(X_i)]}\right] = 1 \]

OLS as Propensity Weight

  • Proposition
  • Proof

Assumption (Linear Propensity Score)

The propensity score of treatment \(D_i\) is a linear function of \(X_i\), namely \[e(X_i) = P(D_i = 1|X_i) = X_i\beta_e\]

Proposition 1 (Equivalence of OLS Estimand and Overlap Weighted IPW)

Suppose the linear propensity assumption holds, the OLS estimator of \(\tau_{ols}\) \[ \tau_{ols} = \tau_{overlap} \]

By the projection or the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem, we have \[ \tau_{ols} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i\tilde{D}_i]}{\mathbb{E}[\tilde{D}_i^2]} \] where \(\tilde{D}_i = D_i - X_i\beta_e\) with \(\beta_e = \mathbb{E}[X'X]^{-1}\mathbb{E}[X'D]\). Then we have \[\begin{aligned} \tau_{ols} &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))]}{\mathbb{E}[(D_i - e(X_i))^2]} \\ &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))]}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \frac{Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \cdot \delta_i\right] = \tau_{overlap} \end{aligned}\]

Comments on Proposition 2


  • Linear propensity score assumptions has been assumed in Aronow and Samii (2016); Abadie et al. (2020); Słoczyński (2022)
    • Also common in empirical studies
    • Not necessarily strong because \(X_i\) can include powers and interactions
    • Inherently satisfied with saturated model
  • OLS is Weighted IPW, thus has a causal interpretation
  • In general, OLS does not identify ATE, unless one of the following holds:
    • Homogeneous treatment effect (constant \(\tau_i\)) or
    • Randomized treatment assignment (constant \(h(X_i)\))

Example: One-way Fixed Effect Model


OWFE is one example of saturated model \[ Y_i = \alpha_s + \tau_{owfe}D_i + \epsilon_i \] where \(\alpha_s\) is strata fixed effect.

Let \(n_{1s} = \sum_{i \in s}D_i\), \(n_{0s} = \sum_{i \in s}1 - D_i\)

Let \(\bar{Y}_{1s}\), \(\bar{Y}_{0s}\) be the sample mean of \(Y_i\) in the treatment and control group in strata \(s\)

\[ \tau_{owfe} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(s)}{\mathbb{E}[h(x)]} \cdot \tau_s \right] \] where \(e(s) = \frac{n_{1s}}{n_{1s} + n_{0s}}\), \(h(s) = e(s)(1 - e(s)) = \frac{n_{1s}n_{0s}}{n_{1s} + n_{0s}}\), \(\tau_s = \bar{Y}_{1s} - \bar{Y}_{0s}\)

Properties of OLS (overlap weight)


  • The purpose of weighting is to achieve similar distribution of \(X_i\) in the treated and control group
  • Let \(f_d(x) = P(X_i = x|D_i = d)\) be the density of \(X_i\) in the treated and control group

Properties of OLS (overlap weight)


From \(h(x)\) to weights on each group

  • By Bayes rule

\[ f_1(x) = \frac{e(x)f(x)}{P(D_i = 1)}, \quad \quad f_0(x) = \frac{(1 - e(x))f(x)}{P(D_i = 0)} \]

  • Let \(f_1(x)g_1(x) = f_0(x)g_0(x) = f(x)h(x)\), again by Bayes rule

\[ g_1(x) \propto h(x)/e(x), \quad \quad g_0(x) \propto h(x)/(1 - e(x)) \]

  • For overlap weights, \((g_1(x), g_0(x)) = (1 - e(x), e(x))\)

Properties of OLS (overlap weight)


Properties:

  1. Undesired weights: Treated and control groups are weighted by probability of being assigned to the opposite group.
    • Słoczyński (2022) weights have similar property
    • Another way to see this is by rearranging \(\tau_{ols}\) \[ \tau_{ols} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[\color{red}(1 - e(X_i))\color{black}D_iY_i - \color{red}e(X_i)\color{black}(1 - D_i)Y_i]}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \]
  2. \(\tau_{ols} \approx\) ATT when \(e(x) \approx 0\) as \((1 - e(X_i), e(X_i)) \approx (1, \frac{e(x)}{1 - e(x)})\); \(\tau_{ols} \approx\) ATU when \(e(x) \approx 1\).

Properties of OLS (overlap weight)


Properties (Li, Morgan, and Zaslavsky 2018):

  1. Efficiency: \(Var(\hat\tau_{ols}) \leqslant Var(\hat\tau_{wipw})\) for all \(h(X_i)\)
  2. Robust to poor overlap:
    • Bounded
    • Maximized with \(e(x) = 0.5\)
    • More weights when \(e(x)\) is close to \(0.5\), less weights when \(e(x)\) away from \(0.5\).
  3. Exact sample balance with logit \(e(x)\)

\[ \frac{\sum_i X_i D_i (1 - \hat{e}(X_i))}{\sum_i D_i (1 - \hat{e}(X_i))} = \frac{\sum_i X_i (1 - D_i) \hat{e}(X_i)}{\sum_i (1 - D_i) \hat{e}(X_i)} \]

Is OLS Without Interactions Doubly Robust?


  • Double robustness means that \(\hat\tau\) is consistent of ATE if either \(\mathbb{E}[D_i|X_i]\) or \(\mathbb{E}[Y_i(d)|X_i]\) is correctly specified
  • Is \(\tau_{ols}\) a doubly robust estimand of overlap ATE?

Why do we care?

  1. Modelling choices
    • How to choose \(X_i\) when we write down the regression equation
  2. Robustness of OLS
    • Kline (2011) shows that OB (full interaction regression) targets ATE and is doubly robust \[ Y_i = \tau_{ob} D_i + X_i \beta + D_i(X_i - \bar{X})\gamma + \epsilon_i \]
    • IPW has a doubly robust version: Augmented IPW (AIPW)(Robins, Rotnitzky, and Zhao 1994)

Is OLS Without Interactions Doubly Robust?


\[ \tau_{ols} = \mathbb{E}\left[h(X_i)(\mu_{1, ols}(X_i) - \mu_{0, ols}(X_i))\right] \] where \[\begin{aligned} &\mu_{1, ols}(X_i) = Y_i(1) + \frac{(D_i - e(X_i)Y_i(1)) - (D_i - e(X_i))e(X_i)(Y_i(1) + \mu_1(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))} \\ &\mu_{0, ols}(X_i) = Y_i(0) + \frac{(D_i - e(X_i)\mu_0(X_i)) - (D_i - e(X_i))e(X_i)(Y_i(0) + \mu_0(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))} \\ &\mu_d(X_i) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(d)|X_i] \end{aligned}\] Therefore, \[\begin{aligned} \tau_{ols} = \tau_{overlap} &+ \mathbb{E}\left[h(X_i) \cdot \frac{(D_i - e(X_i))(Y_i(1) - \mu_0(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}\right] \\ &- \mathbb{E}\left[h(X_i) \cdot \frac{(D_i - e(X_i))e(X_i)(Y_i(1) - Y_i(0) + \mu_1(X_i) - \mu_0(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}\right] \end{aligned}\]

Comments on Robustness of OLS


  • If \(e(X_i)\) is correctly specified but \(\mu_d(X_i)\) is not, \(\tau_{ols} = \tau_{overlap}\)
  • If \(\mu_d(X_i)\) is correctly specified but \(e(X_i)\) is not, \(\tau_{ols} \neq \tau_{overlap}\)


  • Counter-intuitive modelling procedure
    • When writing down the model \(Y_i = \tau_{ols} D_i + X_i \beta + \epsilon_i\)
    • choose \(X_i\) in the propensity model instead of variables that affects \(Y_i\)
  • Identification completely relies on specification of \(\mathbb{E}[D_i|X_i]\)
  • Be careful with linear propensity assumption
    • Simulations results on bias of OLS when propensity is nonlinear

Estimating Weighted ATE: IPW

  • Two commonly used IPW estimators can be adapted to estimate weighted ATE
    • Horvitz-Thompson (HT) type estimator (Hirano, Imbens, and Ridder 2003) \[ \hat\tau^h_{HT} = \sum_{i = 1}^{N} \hat{h}(X_i) \left(\frac{Y_i D_i}{\hat{e}(X_i)} - \frac{Y_i(1 - D_i)}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)}\right) \bigg/ \sum_{i = 1}^{N} \hat{h}(X_i) \]
    • Hajek type estimator (Li, Morgan, and Zaslavsky 2018) \[ \hat\tau^h_{Hajek} = \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^N Y_i D_i \hat{w}_1(X_i)}{\sum_{i = 1}^N D_i \hat{w}_1(X_i)} - \frac{\sum_{i = 1}^N Y_i (1 - D_i) \hat{w}_0(X_i)}{\sum_{i = 1}^N (1 - D_i) \hat{w}_0(X_i)} \] where \(\hat{w}_1(x) = \hat{h}(x) / \hat{e}(x)\) and \(\hat{w}_0(x) = \hat{h}(x) / (1 - \hat{e}(x))\). Both estimators are consistent for \(\tau_{wipw}\) and they are asymptotically equivalent.
  • Proposition 2 shows that the two estimators are both consistent and asymptotically equivalent
  • In finite sample, they are usually numerically different

Estimating Weighted ATE: IPW

Proposition 2 (Influence Function of \(\hat\tau^h_{HT}\) and \(\hat\tau^h_{Hajek}\))

Suppose the unconfoundedness and overlap assumptions hold, \(\hat\tau^h_{HT}\) and \(\hat\tau^h_{Hajek}\) shares the same influence function. Namely, \[\begin{aligned} &\sqrt{n}(\hat\tau^h_{HT} - \tau_{wipw}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 1}^n \psi(Y_i, D_i, X_i) + o_p(1) \\ &\sqrt{n}(\hat\tau^h_{Hajek} - \tau_{wipw}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i = 1}^n \psi(Y_i, D_i, X_i) + o_p(1) \end{aligned}\] with \[\begin{aligned} \psi(y, d, x) = \frac{dh(x)}{\bar{h}e(x)}(y - \mu_1(x)) - \frac{(1 - d)h(x)}{\bar{h}(1 - e(x))}(y - \mu_0(x)) + \frac{h(x)}{\bar{h}} + \frac{(d - e(x))\partial_eh(x)}{\bar{h}}(\tau_{wipw} - \tau_{wate}) \end{aligned}\] where \(\mu_d(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y_i(d)|X_i = x]\) for \(d = 0, 1\), \(\bar{h} = \mathbb{E}[h(X_i)]\) and \(\partial_e h(x)\) is the Gateaux derivative of \(h(x)\) with respect to \(e(x)\).

Estimating ATE: OLS


  • OLS does not identify ATE in general
  • LS estimation of the class of weighted ATE is difficult
  • If a researcher is interested in ATE instead of \(\tau_{overlap}\)
  • She can use IPW or the following two regressions, both are consistent

Weighted Least Squares Estimators of the ATE

  1. Regress \(Y_i\) on \(D_i - \hat{e}(X_i)\) with weight \(\left(\frac{1}{\hat{e}(X_i)} + \frac{1}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)}\right)\)

  2. Regress \(Y_i\) on \(1\) and \(D_i\) with weight \(\left(\frac{D_i}{\hat{e}(X_i)} + \frac{1 - D_i}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)}\right)\)

where \(\hat{e}(x)\) is a consistent estimator of \(e(x)\).

Conclusion


  • When using OLS, change the interpretation of the coefficient
    • ATE \(\rightarrow\) overlap-weighted ATE
    • OLS is not doubly robust and identification depends on specification of the propensity model


  • OLS is inferior to IPW
    • OLS = overlap IPW + linear propensity assumption
    • IPW has more flexibility in terms of specifying the propensity model
    • IPW can easily identify different WATEs
    • AIPW provides more robustness

Example 2: TWFE for \(2 \times 2\) DiD

TWFE estimation of DiD: \[ Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \tau_{twfe} D_{it} + \epsilon_{it} \]

Define \(\bar{Y}_t^c = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{i:C_i = c} Y_{it}\) and \(\hat\tau_t^{cc'} = \bar{Y}_t^{c'} - \bar{Y}_t^c\)

(Decomposition of TWFE estimator of DiD)

\[ \hat\tau_{twfe} = \sum_t \sum_{c \leqslant t} w_t^c \hat\tau_t^{\infty c} + \sum_t \sum_{c > t} w_t^c \hat\tau_{t}^{\infty c} \] with properties \(\sum_t\sum_{c \leqslant t} w_t^c = 1\) and \(\sum_t\sum_{c > t} w_t^c = -1\).

Example 2: TWFE for \(2 \times 2\) DiD

  • The first term: weighted comparison of treated cohort vs never-treated cohort at \(t\)
  • The second term: weighted comparison of not-yet-treated vs never-treated cohort at \(t\)

For \(2 \times 2\) DiD, \(t \in \{1, 2\}\) and \(c \in \{2, \infty\}\)

Under the parallel trend and no anticipation assumption, Proposition 3 implies \[ \hat\tau_{twfe} = -\hat\tau_1^{\infty2} + \hat\tau_2^{\infty2} = (\bar{Y}_2^2 - \bar{Y}_2^\infty) - (\bar{Y}_1^2 - \bar{Y}_1^\infty) \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{E}[Y_{it}(1) - Y_{it}(0)|D_{it} = 1] \]
