OLS Estimator of Treatment Effects

Consider a common scenario in policy evaluation:

For each individual \(i\) we observe

  • A binary treatment/policy \(D_i\)
  • Some outcome variable \(Y_i\)
  • Control variables/Covariates \(X_i\)

To estimate the treatment effect, economists often consider OLS estimator of \(\tau_{ols}\) in \[ Y_i = \tau_{ols} D_i + X_i \beta + \epsilon_i \] and assume Exogeneity \(\mathbb{E}[\epsilon_i|D_i, X_i] = 0\)

OLS Estimators of Treatment Effects

But what is \(\tau_{ols}\)?

  • \(\tau_{ols}\) is ATE in randomized experiments (Imbens and Rubin 2015)
  • \(\tau_{ols}\) is ATE when the treatment effects are homogeneous (Angrist and Pischke 2009)

In the presence of heterogeneous treatment effect

  • \(\tau_{ols}\) is a convex combination of average treatment effect on the treated (ATT) and untreated (ATU) (Słoczyński 2022)
    • Under restrictive assumptions

In this paper, I propose a new interpretation of \(\tau_{ols}\) with less assumptions

The Potential Outcome Framework


The potential outcome framework (Rubin 1974) is the most popular tool for treatment effect analysis

Potential outcomes: \(Y_i(1)\)/\(Y_i(0)\) if unit \(i\) were treated/untreated

Observed outcome: \(Y_i = Y_i(1)D_i + Y_i(0)(1 - D_i)\)

There are two assumptions to identify treatment effects

  1. Unconfoundedness \(D_i \perp (Y_i(1), Y_i(0)) | X_i\)

How is this related to Exogeneity?

  1. Overlap \(0 < P(D_i = 1|X_i) < 1\)

Is this necessary for OLS?

The Literature on OLS

Connection between Exogeneity and Unconfoundedness

  • Imbens (2004): Exogeneity = Unconfoundedness under homogeneous treatment effect

Causal interpretation of \(\tau_{ols}\)

  • Unconfoundedness
    • Angrist (1998): convex combination of ATEs in strata defined by discrete \(X_i\)
    • Aronow and Samii (2016): weighted average of individual treatment effects
      • A slightly different form of unconfoundedness
      • Linear propensity score
      • Overlap
  • Exogeneity
    • Słoczyński (2022): convex combination of ATT and ATU
      • Not clear what exogeneity means in potential outcome framework
      • Linear propensity score
      • \(Y(1)\) and \(Y(0)\) are linear functions of propensity score instead of covariates
      • Overlap

Contribution

Under the potential outcome framework

  • Explore the plausibility of Linear propensity score
    • Usually assumed in the literature on causal interpretation of OLS

Weighted IPW

Define the ATE: \(\tau_{ate} = \mathbb{E}[Y(1) - Y(0)]\)

Under the unconfounded and overlap assumptions,

Let \(e(X_i) = P(D_i = 1|X_i)\) be the propensity score. The identification strategy of IPW is \[ \tau_{ipw} = \mathbb{E}\left[\underbrace{\frac{Y_iD_i}{e(X_i)} - \frac{Y_i(1 - D_i)}{1 - e(X_i)}}_{\tau_i := }\right] = \tau_{ate} \]

Further extended by Hirano, Imbens, and Ridder (2003) for ATE in a subpopulation defined by weight \(h(X_i)\)

\[ \text{Weighted IPW}: \tau_{wipw} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(X_i)}{\mathbb{E}[h(X_i)]} \cdot \tau_i\right] \]

All of weighted IPW estimators have a causal interpretation in the sense that \[ h(X_i) > 0 \ \forall X_i \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(X_i)}{\mathbb{E}[h(X_i)]}\right] = 1 \]

OLS as Propensity Weight

  • Proposition
  • Proof

Assumption (Linear Propensity Score)

The propensity score of treatment \(D_i\) is a linear function of \(X_i\), namely \[e(X_i) = P(D_i = 1|X_i) = X_i\beta_e\]

Proposition 1  

(Equivalence of OLS Estimand and Overlap Weighted IPW)

Suppose the linear propensity assumption holds, the OLS estimator of \(\tau_{ols}\) \[ \tau_{ols} = \tau_{overlap} \]

By the projection or the Frisch-Waugh-Lovell theorem, we have \[ \tau_{ols} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i\tilde{D}_i]}{\mathbb{E}[\tilde{D}_i^2]} \] where \(\tilde{D}_i = D_i - X\beta_e\) with \(\beta_e = \mathbb{E}[X'X]^{-1}\mathbb{E}[X'D]\). Then we have \[\begin{aligned} \tau_{ols} &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))]}{\mathbb{E}[(D_i - e(X_i))^2]} \\ &= \frac{\mathbb{E}[Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))]}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \frac{Y_i(D_i - e(X_i))}{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}\right] \\ &= \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}{\mathbb{E}[e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))]} \cdot \tau_i\right] = \tau_{overlap} \end{aligned}\]

Comments on Proposition 1

  • OLS is implicitly using a linear specification for the propensity score

  • OLS is Weighted IPW, thus has a causal interpretation

  • The only assumption required is linear propensity, the identification of causal effect fully relies on \(\tau_i\)

    • Both unconfoundedness and overlap assumption required

  • In general, OLS does not identify ATE. Extra assumptions required:

    • Homogeneous treatment effect (constant \(\tau_i\)) or
    • Randomized treatment assignment (constant \(h(X_i)\))

As a member of weighted IPW, the overlap weight has been studied by Li, Morgan, and Zaslavsky (2018), we can use their results to learn the properties of OLS estimator of treatment effect

Properties of OLS estimator

— Properties of overlap weight


  • \(Var(\hat\tau_{ols}) \leqslant Var(\hat\tau_{wipw})\) for all \(h(\cdot)\)1

  • Robust to poor overlap: more weights when \(e(x)\) close to \(0.5\), less weights when \(e(x)\) away from \(0.5\).

  • Weights on \(Y_i\): \((1 - e(X_i), e(X_i))\), probability of being assigned to the opposite group.

  • \(\tau_{ols} \approx\) ATT when \(e(x) \approx 0\) as \((1 - e(X_i), e(X_i)) \approx (1, \frac{e(x)}{1 - e(x)})\); \(\tau_{ols} \approx\) ATU when \(e(x) \approx 1\).2

  1. This explains why OLS estimators have smaller standard errors than IPW estimators, while IPW estimators with a consistent estimator of \(e(x)\) is shown to be semiparametrically efficient in Hirano, Imbens, and Ridder (2003)

  2. This suggests to consider ATT as an alternative to ATE or \(\tau_{overlap}\) when the treated group is small. Later used in Application

WLS estimators of ATE

  • OLS does not identify ATE in general
  • If a researcher is interested in ATE instead of \(\tau_{overlap}\)
  • She can use IPW or the following two regressions

Proposition 2  

(Weighted Least Squares Estimators of the ATE)

ATE can be consistently estimated by the following two methods.

  1. Regress \(Y_i\) on \(D_i - \hat{e}(X_i)\) with weight \(\left(\frac{1}{\hat{e}(X_i)} + \frac{1}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)}\right)\)

  2. Regress \(Y_i\) on \(1\) and \(D_i\) with weight \(\left(\frac{D_i}{\hat{e}(X_i)} + \frac{1 - D_i}{1 - \hat{e}(X_i)}\right)\)

where \(\hat{e}(x)\) is a consistent estimator of \(e(x)\).

Violation of Linear Propensity Assumption

Under misspecification of the propensity score, \[ \tau_{ols} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{D_i - l(X_i)}{D_i - e(X_i)} \cdot \frac{e(X_i)(1 - e(X_i))}{\mathbb{E}[(D_i - l(X_i))^2]} \cdot \tau_i\right] \] where \(l(X_i)\) is the best linear projection of the propensity score on the covariates.

Combine the linear propensity and overlap assumption \[ P(0 < X_i\beta_e < 1) = 1 \]

If linear propensity fails to hold, \[ \text{fitted propensity outside} \ [0, 1] \rightarrow \text{negative weights in} \ \tau_{ols} \]

Weights are negative if \(l(X_i) > 1\) and \(D_i = 1\), or if \(l(X_i) < 0\) and \(D_i = 0\)

Violation of Linear Propensity Assumption

  • The problem of negative weights can be serious
  • Often ignored by researchers because the linear regression of \(e(x)\) is done in the background
    • In LaLonde (1986), \(45\%\) of the fitted propensity score is negative
    • In Aizer et al. (2016), \(31\%\) of the fitted propensities in the baseline model is outside \([0, 1]\)
      • Effect of cash transfer to poor family on the longevity of children
      • Cash transfer increases about 1 year of lift expectancy relative to a mean of 72.5

When Linear Propensity is Not a Concern

The propensity score is inherently linear if the propensity score is saturated

Proposition 3  

(One-way fixed effect model)

\[ Y_i = \alpha_s + \tau_{owfe} D_i + \epsilon_i \] where \(\alpha_s\) is the strata fixed effects.

Let \(N_{1s} = \sum_{i \in s}D_i\), \(N_{0s} = \sum_{i \in s}1 - D_i\)

and \(\bar{Y}_{ds}\) be the sample mean of \(Y_i\) in the treatment and control group for \(d = 1, 0\) \[ \tau_{owfe} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{h(s)}{\mathbb{E}[h(s)]}\cdot \tau_s\right] \] where \(e(s) = \frac{N_{1s}}{N_{1s} + N_{0s}}\), \(h(s) = e(s)(1 - e(s))= \frac{N_{1s}N_{0s}}{N_{1s} + N_{0s}}\), \(\tau_s = \bar{Y}_{1s} - \bar{Y}_{0s}\).

The \(h(s)\) is exactly the precision weight that minimizes the variance of treatment effect in stratified experiments (Imbens and Rubin 2015, chap. 9.4)

When Linear Propensity is Not a Concern

The negative weights can be used as a part of identification procedure such as in DiD

  • Proposition 3 does not generalize to two-way fixed effect models (TWFE).
  • Because the two-way fixed effects overlap with each other and thus not mutually exclusive
  • DiD assumptions make use of the negative weights and move the goalpost from \(\tau_{overlap}\) to ATT

Consider the general case of multi-period and multi-group DiD. For each unit \(i\):

  • Initial treatment period \(C_i \in \{1, ..., T, \infty\}\)
  • Realized outcome \(Y_{it} = Y_{it}(C_i)\) with \(C_i = \infty\) represents the units who are never treated.
  • \(D_{it} = 1_{C_i \leqslant t}\)
  • A unit belongs to cohort \(c\) if \(C_i = c\)

When Linear Propensity is Not a Concern

TWFE estimation of DiD: \[ Y_{it} = \alpha_i + \gamma_t + \tau_{twfe} D_{it} + \epsilon_{it} \]

Define \(\bar{Y}_t^c = \frac{1}{N_c}\sum_{i:C_i = c} Y_{it}\) and \(\hat\tau_t^{cc'} = \bar{Y}_t^{c'} - \bar{Y}_t^c\)

Proposition 4  

(Decomposition of TWFE estimator of DiD)

\[ \hat\tau_{twfe} = \sum_t \sum_{c \leqslant t} w_t^c \hat\tau_t^{\infty c} + \sum_t \sum_{c > t} w_t^c \hat\tau_{t}^{\infty c} \] with properties \(\sum_t\sum_{c \leqslant t} w_t^c = 1\) and \(\sum_t\sum_{c > t} w_t^c = -1\).

When Linear Propensity is Not a Concern

  • The first term: weighted comparison of treated cohort vs never-treated cohort at \(t\)
  • The second term: weighted comparison of not-yet-treated vs never-treated cohort at \(t\)

For \(2 \times 2\) DiD, \(t \in \{1, 2\}\) and \(c \in \{2, \infty\}\)

Under the parallel trend and no anticipation assumption, Proposition 4 implies \[ \hat\tau_{twfe} = -\hat\tau_1^{\infty2} + \hat\tau_2^{\infty2} = (\bar{Y}_2^2 - \bar{Y}_2^\infty) - (\bar{Y}_1^2 - \bar{Y}_1^\infty) \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{E}[Y_{it}(1) - Y_{it}(0)|D_{it} = 1] \]

Conclusion and Suggestions

Under the linear propensity assumption

  • Causal interpretation: OLS = overlap IPW, identifies overlap weighted ATE
  • Some nice properties: variance minimizing, more robust to poor overlap than ATE estimators

If linear propensity assumption is violated

  • OLS is non-causal
    • Check the fitted values of propensity score
    • Use weighted IPW with logit propensity score
    • Consider ATT/ATU when propensity is low/high for all units

Linear propensity score is not a concern for

  • One-way fixed effect model, \(2 \times 2\) DiD
    • Use OLS because it is simple
